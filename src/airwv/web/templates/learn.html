Understanding your air

A plain-language guide to what we measure, what the colors mean, how air and water affect your health, how the laws that govern pollution work, and how it all ties to climate.

Why air quality matters here

West Virginia's ridges and river valleys trap air. On calm nights, cooler air settles into the valleys and forms a temperature inversion — a lid that holds pollution near the ground until the next day's sun or wind mixes it out. Add traffic, wood and coal home heating, and heavy industry along the river corridors — the Kanawha Valley's "Chemical Valley" stretch is a well-known example — and the same spot can read clean at midday and hazy by dawn.

Our own sensors show it: along the industrial corridor, fine particles build up overnight (roughly 1.4–2× the daytime level) while an upriver control site stays flat. Smoke can also drift in from far away — regional wildfires have pushed WV into "unhealthy" days. Dense, always-on community sensors are exactly what it takes to catch these local, hour-by-hour swings that a handful of official monitors can miss.

On calm nights, warm air settles over cooler valley air and acts as a lid — pollution can't rise past it, so it pools near the ground until the sun or wind breaks the inversion. It's why the same spot can read clean at midday and hazy by dawn.

What we measure

PM2.5 — fine particles

Particles 2.5 micrometers or smaller — about 30× thinner than a hair. They come from anything that burns: vehicles, industry, wood stoves, wildfires. Because they're so small they slip deep into the lungs and into the bloodstream, which is why PM2.5 is the pollutant most tied to health effects. It's the number our map is colored by.

PM10 — coarse particles

Bigger particles up to 10 µm: road and construction dust, pollen, mold. Irritating to eyes, nose, and throat, and hard on people with asthma.

PM1.0 — ultrafine

The smallest fraction we report — largely combustion-related and useful for spotting fresh smoke or exhaust.

VOCs — volatile organic compounds

Gases released by fuels, solvents, and many industrial processes; often what you smell. Our community sensors report a single relative gas index — good for spotting changes and odor events, not a calibrated measure of any specific compound.

Ozone — summer smog

Ground-level ozone isn't emitted directly — it forms in sunlight when VOCs and nitrogen oxides react, so it peaks on hot, sunny afternoons. It irritates the lungs and worsens asthma. Community PurpleAir sensors can't measure ozone, so we pull it from EPA AirNow reference monitors — a sparser, reference-only layer you can chart on the Air page.

Drawn to scale. PM2.5 is roughly 30× smaller than a human hair — small enough to slip past your body's defenses into the lungs and bloodstream. (Hair thickness varies a lot from person to person — roughly 17–180 µm depending on hair type — so "~70 µm" is just a common reference point. The takeaway holds for everyone: PM2.5 is far too small to see or feel.)

What's actually in "VOCs"

"VOC" is an umbrella for hundreds of carbon-based gases. Our low-cost sensors can't tell them apart — they report one combined index. But the individual compounds behind that number matter a lot for health, and EPA's air-toxics network measures many of them one by one. Common ones in industrial and traffic settings:

  • Benzene — fuels, vehicle exhaust, chemical plants; a known human carcinogen (linked to leukemia).
  • Toluene, ethylbenzene, xylenes — with benzene these are the "BTEX" group from fuels and solvents; nervous-system and respiratory irritants.
  • Formaldehyde — combustion and building materials; a carcinogen and strong irritant.
  • 1,3-Butadiene — combustion and synthetic-rubber production; a carcinogen.
  • Acrolein — combustion; an intense respiratory irritant even at low levels.
  • Methylene chloride, perchloroethylene ("perc") — degreasers and dry-cleaning solvents.
  • Styrene — plastics and resins manufacturing.

Many VOCs also react in sunlight to form ground-level ozone, a separate lung irritant. Our sensor's index can't identify or quantify these compounds — for actual concentrations you'd need EPA/lab air-toxics sampling.

What our VOC sensor actually detects

To be clear about the hardware: the PurpleAir units in this network sense gases with a low-cost metal-oxide (MOX) gas sensor (a Bosch BME680-class chip). It doesn't identify molecules — its surface resistance changes in the presence of reducing gases as a group: many VOCs (alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, aromatics like the BTEX above) plus things like carbon monoxide and hydrogen. What comes out is one relative "gas resistance" number, sensitive to temperature and humidity, reported as a single index.

So honestly: it's a "something changed" detector, not a benzene meter. A rise means more reducing gas in the air near that sensor — a reason to look closer or report an odor, not a measurement of any one chemical. Pinning down which compound (and how much) takes EPA/lab air-toxics sampling — the kind a confirmed community report can help trigger.

Who's most at risk

Children, adults over 65, pregnant people, and anyone with asthma, COPD, or heart disease feel air pollution first and hardest. Symptoms can include coughing, throat and eye irritation, shortness of breath, and tightness in the chest.

What you can do

  • Check before you go. On elevated days, move hard exercise indoors or to cleaner hours (afternoons often clear the overnight valley buildup).
  • Clean your indoor air. A HEPA purifier — or a box-fan filter — makes a real difference during smoke or inversion events. Keep windows closed when it's bad.
  • Mask up when it's smoky. A well-fitted N95/KN95 filters fine particles; cloth and surgical masks don't.
  • Speak up. Smell or see something? Report a concern → to put it on the map, and report pollution or spills to WV DEP (see the Laws & Permits tab for how complaints and violations work).

Beyond PM2.5 — the other exposures we track

Ground-level ozone is a lung irritant that forms on hot, sunny afternoons. It can trigger asthma, tighten the chest, and inflame airways even in healthy people — which is why "code orange/red" ozone days matter most for kids and anyone with lung disease.

VOCs are a mixed bag: some (like benzene and formaldehyde, common around oil & gas) are known human carcinogens; others mainly cause short-term headaches, dizziness, and eye/throat irritation. Our community VOC reading is a relative gas index — it can flag that something is off, but not which chemical or at what concentration. Persistent VOC odors near a facility or well are worth reporting so they can be looked at properly.

🕳️ Gas wells & hydrogen sulfide (H2S) — the rotten-egg hazard

West Virginia's thousands of abandoned and orphaned gas wells can leak raw natural gas and hydrogen sulfide (H2S) — a toxic gas with a rotten-egg smell. This is the issue behind the Rutledge neighborhood in Charleston, and a founding reason communities here started monitoring their own air. (Turn on the 🛢️ Abandoned wells map layer to see the wells near you — orphans in red.)

⚠️ Raw natural gas is odorless. The "gas smell" you know from a kitchen stove is a chemical (mercaptan) that utilities add on purpose so you can detect leaks. Gas venting straight from a well has no such additive — it's mostly methane, which is colorless and has no smell. So a leaking well can be releasing gas you'll never notice by nose. That's exactly why an air monitor matters: our community VOC index responds to the hydrocarbons your senses can't, giving a signal where there'd otherwise be none. (H2S, when it's in the mix, does smell — but see below.)

The dangerous part: you can smell H2S at very low levels, but at higher concentrations it deadens your sense of smell within minutes — so the smell going away does not mean the gas is gone. Never treat "I don't smell it anymore" as safe.

  • Lower-level, near-home exposure — what residents have reported: headaches, nausea, dizziness, fatigue, eye/throat irritation, trouble sleeping, and skin rashes.
  • Higher levels can cause rapid dizziness, loss of consciousness, and are life-threatening — leave the area immediately and get to fresh air.

What to do: if you smell persistent gas or rotten eggs, don't ignore it — get to fresh air, and for a strong or sudden smell call 911 (or your gas utility's emergency line). Then put it on the map: click the well and 🚨 report the problem →, and see the Events page for the plugging backlog. Health info: CDC/ATSDR ToxFAQs — Hydrogen Sulfide.

💧 In your water — what the contaminants do

The Water page tracks measured stream chemistry, coal-mine discharges, and Safe Drinking Water Act violations by system. Here's why those numbers matter for your body:

  • Lead — no safe level, especially for young children (affects brain development). Usually comes from old pipes/plumbing, not the source water.
  • Nitrate — dangerous for bottle-fed infants ("blue baby syndrome"); often from agriculture or sewage.
  • Bacteria / E. coli — signals fecal contamination; causes GI illness, and spikes after heavy rain and combined-sewer overflows.
  • Selenium, iron, manganese — mining/acid-drainage markers. Selenium bioaccumulates in fish (a fish-consumption concern); manganese at high levels is a neurodevelopment concern for kids.

If your water looks, smells, or tastes off, contact your water system and report it →. For a boil-water advisory, follow your utility's guidance. Sources: EPA drinking-water standards; CDC; WV DHHR.

Where your drinking water comes from

Most West Virginians drink surface water — rivers and streams — not well water. Charleston's tap water, for example, comes primarily from the Elk River, treated at West Virginia American Water's Kanawha Valley plant, and that supply ultimately depends on thousands of small mountain streams upstream. Because the intake sits on a river, whatever happens upstream reaches your tap — exactly what the 2014 Freedom Industries spill exposed: the leak was barely a mile and a half above the intake, and 300,000 people lost usable water within hours. Knowing your source — and what sits upstream of the intake — is the first step in protecting it.

Find your own system's source and test results in its annual Consumer Confidence Report (every water utility must publish one), or in WV's source-water assessments.

It's the system, not just the polluters — combined sewer overflows

Many older WV towns (including parts of Charleston) still have combined sewers that carry stormwater and sewage in the same pipe. In dry weather it all goes to treatment — but during heavy rain the system overflows by design, discharging untreated sewage straight into the river (a "combined sewer overflow," or CSO). That's an aging- infrastructure problem, not a single bad actor, and it's a major reason river bacteria spike after storms. Fixing it is a systems question — one worth understanding alongside individual polluters.

Can you swim or boat in it?

It depends on where and when. In urban stretches like the Kanawha, the main swimming risk usually isn't a chemical — it's bacteria (E. coli) from sewage and runoff, which spikes for a day or two after rain (see CSOs above); high E. coli signals fecal pollution and possible pathogens. Charleston Waterkeeper runs a volunteer Swim Alert program that samples bacteria and posts whether it's safe — the best local guide. Rules of thumb: avoid water-contact recreation for ~48 hours after heavy rain, steer clear of stretches right below industrial outfalls, and heed any WV DEP advisory.

Can you eat the fish?

Often yes, in moderation — but check the advisory first. WV publishes a Sport Fish Consumption Advisory: the statewide baseline is no more than one meal per week of most sport fish (rainbow trout excepted), and for some species in some waters (certain bass, catfish) as few as one or two meals per month. The limits are driven mostly by mercury (widespread — it falls from the air, partly from coal, and builds up in fish) and by PCBs and dioxin (more localized, legacy industry). Children and pregnant people should be most cautious. Check the current, water-specific advisory: WV DHHR fish advisories.

How to read the river maps

WV DEP's Integrated Report flags a stream as "impaired" (the Clean Water Act 303(d) list) when it fails a water-quality standard for one of its designated uses — drinking, recreation, or aquatic life. Crucially, a stream can be listed for one specific reason (bacteria, low pH from acid mine drainage, iron, sediment, or "biological" impairment) while being fine for others. So "impaired" doesn't mean "toxic everywhere" — it means "fails a specific standard for a specific use." A lot of WV segments are listed, largely from historic mining and old infrastructure; that's the case for cleaning them up, not for writing them off.

Where air and water meet

They're not separate problems. The same facilities often hold both air and water permits. Mercury emitted into the air settles into rivers and accumulates in fish — which is why an air pollutant drives fish advisories. Rain washes air pollution and land contamination into streams. And the same valleys that trap air pollution (see Basics) tend to concentrate the industry that affects both. Watching air and water together — as this project does — gives a fuller picture than either alone.

Community sensors vs. reference monitors

Low-cost community sensors give us dense, real-time coverage that the sparse network of regulatory monitors can't. The trade-off is precision: they can read a little high, especially in humidity. So we continuously compare each community sensor against the nearest EPA reference monitor and apply a standard EPA correction — you can see all of that on the Air page's validation panel. For official air-quality information, see AirNow.gov.

A note on VOC readings

Our PM2.5 numbers can be validated and corrected against EPA monitors. VOC cannot. The EPA reference monitors we use measure only PM2.5 — no VOC — so there's nothing to calibrate our community VOC against. And the community VOC is a relative index (a single gas-sensor number), not a concentration. Treat VOC as community-only and directional: great for spotting a change or an odor event, not for an exact value or a specific compound.

The Clean Air Act — how air is regulated

The federal Clean Air Act (1970, major amendments 1977 & 1990) is the backbone of U.S. air regulation. Key pieces:

  • NAAQS — EPA sets national health-based limits (National Ambient Air Quality Standards) for six criteria pollutants: PM2.5/PM10, ground-level ozone, carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, nitrogen dioxide, and lead.
  • Attainment vs. nonattainment — areas that meet a standard are "attainment"; those that exceed it are "nonattainment" and must take extra steps to clean up.
  • State Implementation Plans (SIPs) — each state (here, WV DEP's Division of Air Quality) writes an EPA-approved plan to meet the standards.
  • Air toxics (HAPs) — a separate list of 180+ hazardous air pollutants (benzene, formaldehyde, mercury…) controlled by technology-based standards, apart from the criteria pollutants above.

How permits work

A facility that emits above certain thresholds generally can't build or operate without a permit — a legally binding document that sets its limits and obligations:

  • Construction permits (New Source Review / PSD) — required before building or expanding a major source; force modern pollution controls up front.
  • Title V operating permits — the ongoing operating license for major sources. It collects emission limits, required monitoring, recordkeeping, and reporting into one enforceable document.
  • Water: NPDES permits — see the Clean Water Act below.

Permits are public records, usually with a public-comment period before they're issued. They're where the abstract law becomes a concrete number a specific plant must stay under.

What counts as a violation

A violation is a failure to follow the law or a permit — for example:

  • emitting or discharging more than a permit limit allows;
  • operating without a required permit, or building before getting one;
  • failing to monitor, record, or report as the permit requires;
  • violating a national standard (a NAAQS-related rule or an air-toxics standard).

Violations surface through inspections, a facility's own required reports, or monitoring data. Regulators can respond with a Notice of Violation, a consent order, civil penalties/fines, or — for knowing, serious harm — criminal charges. You can look up a facility's compliance and enforcement history on EPA's ECHO database.

The Clean Water Act — the water side

The Clean Water Act (1972) governs pollutant discharges into rivers, streams, and other "waters of the United States":

  • NPDES permits — anyone discharging pollutants from a "point source" (a pipe, outfall, ditch) into a waterway needs a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System permit, which sets effluent limits and monitoring.
  • Water quality standards — states set the conditions a given river must meet for its uses (drinking, recreation, aquatic life); WV DEP's Division of Water & Waste Management administers them here.
  • Violations (over-limit discharges, unpermitted discharges, spills) work much like the air side — NOVs, orders, and penalties, tracked in ECHO.

Chemicals, toxins & contaminated land

Air and water permits aren't the whole story. A separate set of laws governs the chemicals themselves — making them, storing them, dumping them, and cleaning them up:

  • TSCA — the Toxic Substances Control Act lets EPA review and restrict industrial chemicals (it's the authority now phasing down PFAS "forever chemicals" and that banned most uses of asbestos).
  • RCRA — the Resource Conservation & Recovery Act governs hazardous waste "cradle to grave": how it's stored, transported, and disposed of, so it doesn't end up in soil and groundwater.
  • CERCLA / Superfund — the cleanup law for already-contaminated sites. It can force the responsible parties to pay for remediation — and where they can't be found, a federal fund covers it.
  • EPCRA / Right-to-Know — the Emergency Planning & Community Right-to-Know Act is why the Toxics Release Inventory (our Sources page) is public at all: facilities must report the chemicals they store and release.

What pollution does to land & property

Beyond health, contamination has real effects on the environment and on what you own:

  • Soil & groundwater — spills, leaking tanks, mine drainage, and buried waste can leave heavy metals or solvents in the ground for decades, tainting wells and gardens.
  • Ecosystems — acid mine drainage and selenium can render a stream unable to support fish; air deposition and runoff carry harm well beyond the fence line.
  • Property — contamination can lower value, complicate a sale (buyers and lenders may require a Phase I/II environmental assessment), and in serious cases make land unusable until it's remediated.

If you're affected — your recourse

  • Report it — to WV DEP and/or EPA (numbers below); regulators can inspect, issue violations, and order cleanup.
  • Public participation — permits have comment periods, and you can request records under open-records / FOIA laws to see a facility's filings.
  • Citizen suits — the Clean Air and Clean Water Acts let citizens sue violators (or a regulator that fails to act) after proper notice — an important backstop when enforcement lags.
  • Private claims — nuisance, trespass, or "toxic tort" claims for property damage or health harm are handled through the courts with an attorney.

This is plain-language education, not legal advice. For your situation, talk to WV DEP/EPA and, for private claims, a licensed attorney.

Quick glossary

Criteria pollutant

One of the six pollutants with a national health-based limit (NAAQS).

HAP / air toxic

A hazardous air pollutant (e.g., benzene) on EPA's separate 180+ list.

Point source

A single identifiable source of pollution — a smokestack or a discharge pipe.

Effluent limit

The maximum amount of a pollutant a facility may discharge to water.

NOV

Notice of Violation — a formal finding that a facility broke a rule or permit.

TRI

Toxics Release Inventory — public data on facilities' reported chemical releases (the basis of our Sources page).

Superfund (CERCLA)

The federal program that funds and forces cleanup of contaminated sites.

RCRA

The law governing how hazardous waste is stored, hauled, and disposed of — "cradle to grave."

Citizen suit

A lawsuit the Clean Air/Water Acts let ordinary people file against a violator (after notice).

Air quality and climate are the same fire

Most of what harms local air also drives climate change — they come from the same smokestacks and tailpipes. Burning coal, oil, and gas releases fine particles, sulfur dioxide, and nitrogen oxides (the health pollutants we track) and carbon dioxide and methane (the gases that warm the planet). That overlap is good news: cutting fossil-fuel pollution cleans the air you breathe today and slows warming — what scientists call a co-benefit.

The greenhouse basics

Greenhouse gases — chiefly CO₂ from burning fossil fuels and methane from gas systems, coal, and agriculture — trap heat in the atmosphere. Human activity has already warmed the planet about 1.1–1.2 °C above pre-industrial levels, driving more extreme heat, heavier rainfall and flooding, and longer wildfire seasons — including the Canadian wildfire smoke that reached West Virginia (see our Events page).

The Paris Agreement — the global goal

Under the 2015 Paris Agreement, nearly every country agreed to hold global warming to well below 2 °C above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit it to 1.5 °C. Each country sets its own pledge — a Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) — and many, including the U.S., have set goals of net-zero emissions by around 2050.

What scientists say it takes

The IPCC — the UN body that synthesizes the world's climate science — is specific about the 1.5 °C path:

  • Global greenhouse-gas emissions must peak before 2025;
  • fall roughly 43% below 2019 levels by 2030 (and ~60% by 2035);
  • and reach net-zero CO₂ by the early 2050s.

Getting there means rapid, deep cuts across every sector: phasing down unabated fossil-fuel use, scaling up renewables and efficiency, electrifying transport and buildings, sharply reducing methane leaks, protecting forests, and removing some carbon that's already been emitted. The IPCC's finding is blunt but hopeful — the tools exist and are increasingly affordable; the gap is the pace of action. Current national pledges, if met, still leave the world short of 1.5 °C.

What it means for West Virginia

WV's economy has long been tied to coal and, now, natural gas. The energy transition is a real and complicated shift here — but it's also an opportunity: the same changes that cut carbon (cleaner power, less methane leakage, efficiency) directly reduce the PM2.5, SO₂, and VOCs that affect local health. Tracking our own air is one way to make those trade-offs visible and hold the improvements accountable.
